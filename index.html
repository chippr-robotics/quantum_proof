Understanding the Zero-Knowledge Proof for Quantum Circuit Verification

Based on "Securing Elliptic Curve Cryptocurrencies against Quantum Vulnerabilities" by Babbush, Zalcman, Gidney et al. (Google Quantum AI, 2026)

1. The Problem: Trust Without Disclosure

Google Quantum AI claims to have compiled quantum circuits that can break 256-bit Elliptic Curve Discrete Logarithm Problem (ECDLP) over the secp256k1 curve — the cryptographic foundation of Bitcoin, Ethereum, and most major blockchains — with dramatically fewer resources than previously published:

VariantLogical QubitsToffoli Gates
Low-Qubit≤ 1,200≤ 90 million
Low-Gate≤ 1,450≤ 70 million

These estimates represent roughly an order-of-magnitude improvement in spacetime volume over the best prior work. On a superconducting architecture with 10-3 physical error rates, these circuits could execute in under 25 minutes using fewer than half a million physical qubits.

But here is the tension: publishing the actual circuit details would hand adversaries a blueprint for attacking cryptocurrency systems. Yet claiming improved estimates without evidence is indistinguishable from FUD (Fear, Uncertainty, and Doubt). The paper resolves this by applying responsible disclosure via a zero-knowledge proof.

2. What Exactly Is Being Proved?

The zero-knowledge proof demonstrates a precise claim: the authors possess a classical reversible circuit (called a kickmix circuit) that:

  1. Correctly computes elliptic curve point addition on secp256k1
  2. Fits within the claimed resource bounds (qubits and gates)
  3. Passes correctness testing on 9,024 pseudo-random inputs
Why point addition? Elliptic curve point addition is the core bottleneck of Shor's algorithm for ECDLP. The full ECDLP circuit performs a sequence of windowed point additions controlled by phase estimation registers. Given the cost of a single point addition, the total ECDLP cost follows directly from known formulas.

From Point Addition to Full ECDLP Cost

The total non-Clifford (Toffoli) gate count for the n-bit ECDLP circuit is:

ECDLPToff = (PAToff + 3 × 2w) × (2n/w − 4)

And the total qubit count is:

ECDLPQubits = PAQubits + w

where PAToff and PAQubits are the point addition costs, and w is the window size. With the optimal window size w = 16, the 256-bit ECDLP algorithm performs 28 windowed point additions (2 × 256/16 − 4 = 28).

3. The Verification Pipeline

The proof is constructed through a multi-layered pipeline that transforms the claim "we have a good circuit" into a publicly verifiable cryptographic artifact.

Secret Circuit C | v +------------------+ | SHA-256 Hash |----> Public commitment (circuit fingerprint) +------------------+ | v +------------------+ | SHAKE256 XOF |----> 9,024 pseudo-random test inputs | (Fiat-Shamir) | (deterministically derived from circuit hash) +------------------+ | v +------------------+ | Kickmix |----> Simulate circuit on all test inputs | Simulator |----> Count non-Clifford gates (CCX + CCZ) | |----> Verify all outputs match expected point additions +------------------+ | v +------------------+ | Assert Resource |----> Qubits ≤ bound, Toffoli gates ≤ bound | Bounds | +------------------+ | v +------------------+ | SP1 zkVM |----> Arithmetize entire execution into STARK proof | (RISC-V) |----> Recursive composition across shards +------------------+ | v +------------------+ | Groth16 SNARK |----> Final succinct zero-knowledge proof | Wrapper |----> Publicly verifiable, ~256 bytes +------------------+

4. Key Components Explained

4.1 Kickmix Circuits

The secret circuits are kickmix circuits — a specific subclass of quantum circuits composed entirely of:

  • Classical reversible logic gates (NOT, CNOT, Toffoli/CCX, CCZ)
  • Measurement-based uncomputation (MBUC) via Pauli-X basis measurements
  • Diagonal phasing gates for phase correction after measurement

By restricting to this gate set, the circuits never generate intractable quantum entanglement and can be efficiently simulated classically. This is crucial: it means the ZK proof verifier program can simulate the circuit inside a classical virtual machine (the SP1 zkVM).

Measurement-Based Uncomputation (MBUC) Traditional reversible computation requires running the inverse circuit U† to clean up ancilla qubits, doubling the gate cost. MBUC instead measures the ancilla in the Pauli-X basis. With 50% probability, the qubit is cleanly disentangled. Otherwise, a phase kickback occurs that can be corrected with a cheaper phase-correction circuit. This technique significantly reduces non-Clifford gate counts.

4.2 Fiat-Shamir Fuzz Testing

The circuit need not be exactly correct — Shor's algorithm tolerates a small fraction of incorrect outputs. If 1% of inputs produce wrong results, the algorithm fails at most 1% of the time.

To prove approximate correctness, the authors use fuzz testing with the Fiat-Shamir heuristic:

  1. Seed SHAKE256 (a cryptographic extendable-output function) with the raw bytes of the secret circuit
  2. Generate 9,024 pseudo-random test points as G · k where G is the curve generator and k is a random 256-bit integer
  3. Simulate the circuit on all test inputs and verify each output
Why 9,024 tests provide 128-bit security If the circuit were wrong on more than 1% of inputs, the probability of passing all 9,024 independent random tests is at most (1 − 0.01)9024 ≈ 2-130. This is astronomically unlikely — equivalent to 128+ bits of cryptographic security that the circuit is at least 99% correct.

The Fiat-Shamir transform is critical because the test inputs are deterministically derived from the circuit itself. This prevents the prover from crafting a circuit that only works on pre-selected inputs.

4.3 SP1 zkVM Execution

The entire verification program — the kickmix simulator, Fiat-Shamir test generation, secp256k1 point addition logic, and resource counting — is implemented in standard Rust and compiled to RISC-V bytecode by the SP1 zero-knowledge virtual machine.

Inside the zkVM, the guest program performs four tasks:

  1. Commit: Compute SHA-256 hash of the input circuit and publish it as a public output, along with the resource bounds
  2. Generate tests: Use SHAKE256 XOF seeded with the circuit bytes to produce 9,024 Fiat-Shamir test inputs
  3. Simulate & verify: Run the kickmix simulator on all test cases, recording non-Clifford gate counts
  4. Assert bounds: Check that the circuit's qubit count and average Toffoli count satisfy the claimed limits

SP1 records the CPU state at every clock cycle, divides the multi-billion-cycle execution into shards (~222 RISC-V instructions each), generates a STARK proof per shard, and uses recursive proof composition to compress them into a single proof.

4.4 Groth16 SNARK Wrapper

The compressed STARK proof is then wrapped in a Groth16 zk-SNARK to achieve two critical properties:

  • Zero-Knowledge: The proof reveals nothing about the secret circuit beyond what is stated (individual STARK proofs in SP1 do not currently satisfy the ZK property on their own)
  • Succinctness: The final proof is compact and fast to verify
A practical irony The Groth16 SNARK relies on pairing-friendly elliptic curves, making its soundness inherently vulnerable to the very quantum attacks analyzed in the paper. A sufficiently large quantum computer could forge this proof. However, since CRQCs do not yet exist, the proof's soundness remains intact today.

6. What Does This Mean for Cryptocurrencies?

Using standard superconducting hardware assumptions (planar degree-4 connectivity, 10-3 error rates, 10μs control reaction time):

MetricEstimate
Physical qubits required< 500,000
Full ECDLP solve time18–23 minutes
"Primed" on-spend attack time~9 minutes
Reduction vs. prior estimates~20x fewer physical qubits

The ~9-minute attack time is close to Bitcoin's 10-minute average block time, giving on-spend attacks a ~41% success probability against Bitcoin transactions under idealized conditions. Shorter block-time chains fare better: Litecoin (~3%), Zcash (<0.1%), Dogecoin (<0.01%).
